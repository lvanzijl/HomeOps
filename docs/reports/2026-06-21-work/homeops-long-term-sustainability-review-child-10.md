# HomeOps Long-Term Sustainability Review — Child Persona Age 10

## Independent Verdict
HomeOps **mostly** saves effort. I would keep using it if it helps me own my responsibilities and if the goals still feel connected to what I actually do. I would stop caring if it becomes a parent checklist with old tasks.

## Section 1 — Daily Friction Assessment
- **Actions required:** Check my tasks, finish chores or homework routine items, mark them done, maybe see progress.
- **Maintenance required:** I can handle some ownership, but I do not want to organize the whole family system.
- **Data entry required:** Low for completing tasks; medium if I help add goals or shopping items.
- **Repetitive:** Daily chores and routine tasks.
- **Unnecessary-feeling:** Tasks that are already obvious, old chores, or goals that do not change.

**Determine:** A) **Reduce effort** when it gives me a clear plan.

### Strengths
- Makes my contribution visible.
- Helps me remember without being reminded as much.
- One personal goal is easy to understand.

### Weaknesses
- Repeated chores can feel like nagging.
- If progress does not reflect real effort, I may stop trusting it.

**Reviewer Verdict:** Good if I get real ownership.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 2 — Recurring Task Assessment
Recurring tasks are useful for chores, room cleaning, homework habits, and weekly responsibilities. Hygiene is important because I do not want old missed tasks making it look like I failed forever.

- **Daily chores:** Good if there are not too many.
- **Weekly chores:** Good for ownership.
- **Monthly chores:** Okay but less motivating.

### Long-Term Strengths
- Builds habits.
- Reduces parent reminders.
- Keeps current work separate from old misses.

### Long-Term Weaknesses
- Same chores can become boring.
- If cleanup hides context, I may not know what happened.

**Reviewer Verdict:** Good for independence.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 3 — Goal Hygiene Assessment
One individual goal is good because I know what I am working toward. One family goal is also good because we can all remember it. The limit might feel restrictive only if I have two real priorities at once, but too many goals would be worse.

- **Keep active:** Yes, if I helped choose it.
- **Ignore:** If it was chosen for me or is stale.
- **Archive:** Yes, when it is done or no longer important.
- **Abandon:** If progress feels fake or parent-controlled.

**Reviewer Verdict:** Focus helps long-term interest.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 4 — Shopping Assessment
Shopping matters less to me, but I can contribute by adding items or helping with a store list. Grouping is useful if I am shopping with a parent.

- **Weekly shopping:** Useful for family.
- **Multi-store usage:** Good if it learns correctly.
- **Trustworthiness:** Good if items disappear only when actually handled.

### What Becomes Annoying After Months
- Wrong store groups.
- Duplicate items.
- Old completed items cluttering the list.

**Reviewer Verdict:** Good family utility; moderate personal engagement.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 5 — Template Assessment
Morning and Bedtime routines become **setup tools** after a while. Homework Routine can remain a **long-term workflow tool** during school because it helps structure repeated work.

**Reviewer Verdict:** Useful if templates evolve into personal routine ownership rather than parent instructions.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 6 — Stale Data Risk Assessment
### What Accumulates
- Old tasks.
- Old goals.
- Old memories and Helpful Moments.
- Shopping history.

### Highest-Risk Areas
1. Goals I did not choose.
2. Tasks that are no longer relevant.
3. Memories that feel generic.

### Lowest-Risk Areas
1. Current personal goal.
2. Today’s tasks.
3. Active shopping list.

**Reviewer Verdict:** Stale data matters because it makes the app feel less real.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 7 — Maintenance Burden Assessment
After one year, I could maintain my own tasks and maybe my goal, but I would not want to clean all old family data.

### Biggest Maintenance Risks
- Too much parent control.
- Old tasks making progress feel unfair.
- Repetitive recognition.

### Biggest Maintenance Wins
- I can own a clear goal.
- I can see how I helped.
- Fewer reminders from parents.

**Parent burden:** Moderate.  
**Child burden:** Low to moderate if ownership is age-appropriate.

**Reviewer Verdict:** Sustainable if kids can own a small part.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 8 — Input Friction Assessment
- **Tasks:** Completing is easy; adding should stay simple.
- **Shopping:** Easy if just item names.
- **Goals:** Better if chosen with me, not edited often.
- **Helpful Moments:** I like seeing specific moments; I do not want forced writing.

### Would Users Avoid Entering Data?
I would avoid anything that feels like homework. I would use quick task completion and simple goal progress.

**Reviewer Verdict:** Good if contribution does not require typing essays.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 9 — Trust Assessment
I trust HomeOps if it reflects what I actually did. If old tasks or stale goals remain, I will think it is just a parent tracking system.

### Trust Strengths
- Recurring hygiene prevents unfair backlog.
- One goal is clear.
- Helpful Moments can prove real contribution.

### Trust Risks
- Generic recognition.
- Old chores.
- Goals that do not match my current interests.

**One-Year Trust Verdict:** Probably, if goals and tasks are reviewed sometimes.  
**Score:** Good

## Section 10 — Biggest Remaining Problem
The biggest threat is **losing ownership**. If the system becomes old parent-managed lists, I will not care.

**Existing area to improve next:** A weekly review where I can help choose what stays active, what goal matters, and what contribution gets remembered.

## Final Independent Answer
HomeOps **mostly** saves effort. It helps me be responsible, but it has to stay current and let me own my part.
