# HomeOps Long-Term Usage Review — Child Persona Age 6

## Reviewer Lens
This independent review evaluates fun, pride, recognition, and repeated engagement after three months of use.

## Section 1 — 30 Day Review

### Used Weekly
- Child Workspace when a parent says, “Look at your page.”
- Helpful Moments when the child gets praised.
- Celebrations when something fun is close or ready.
- Family Goal when it is easy to understand.

### Already Fading
- Task lists if they look like parent instructions.
- Old memories unless they are shown with excitement.
- Individual progress if the child cannot connect it to something concrete.

### Positive Surprises
- The child likes seeing their own name/avatar and being thanked.
- The child feels proud when help is connected to the family.
- Celebration anticipation can be exciting if the celebration is real and near.

### Repetitive
- Seeing the same chores every day.
- Being told to check the page when nothing new happened.
- Progress text that is too abstract.

### Repeated Value
- “My family noticed me.”
- “I helped.”
- “We are getting closer to something fun.”

### Habits Forming
- Visiting after a parent praises them.
- Checking when a celebration is close.
- Looking at the page with an adult.

### Habits Failing
- Independent daily checking.
- Reading old content.
- Caring about routine task details without parent involvement.

### Strengths
- Recognition creates pride.
- Child Workspace feels personal.
- Celebration gives a reason to come back.

### Weaknesses
- The child needs novelty or adult framing.
- Static pages become invisible quickly.
- Too many words reduce engagement.

### Reviewer Verdict
At 30 days, the six-year-old likes HomeOps when it shows praise, progress, and celebration. They do not treat it as a self-directed productivity tool.

## Section 2 — 90 Day Review

### What Survives
- Helpful Moments that feel special.
- Celebration status when the family is close to something fun.
- Child Workspace as “my page” if it changes enough.
- Family Goal if parents talk about it.

### What Gets Abandoned
- Routine task checking without parent guidance.
- Old memories that are not visually or emotionally highlighted.
- Generic progress updates.

### Background Noise
- Repeated chores.
- Old praise cards.
- Progress numbers that do not feel immediate.

### Family Ritual
- Looking together when the child helped.
- Celebrating a family goal.
- Parent saying, “Look what we noticed.”

### Household Work
For the child, HomeOps is not work unless it is framed as chores. For parents, keeping the child page meaningful is work.

### Trusted
The child trusts the page if it contains real praise and real family celebration, not just instructions.

### Features Likely Still Used
Child Workspace, Helpful Moments, Celebration, simple Family Goal.

### Features Likely Abandoned
Independent task review, old Celebration Memory browsing.

### Reviewer Verdict
At 90 days, the child still visits if an adult makes the page feel alive and proud.

## Section 5 — Child Perspective Age 6

### Child Workspace
Still loved when it feels like “my page.” It fades when it becomes a chore list.

### Helpful Moments
Still loved because appreciation is concrete. “We noticed” matters more than points.

### Celebration
Still exciting if the celebration is understandable and soon. If it is too far away, it fades.

### Family Goal
Works if simple: “We are all helping to get to movie night.” It fails if abstract.

### What Still Feels Fun
- Avatar/name identity.
- Seeing appreciation.
- Seeing a celebration get closer.
- Showing the page to a parent.

### What Becomes Invisible
- Old cards.
- Repeated chores.
- Long explanations.
- Progress that does not change.

### What Creates Pride
- Specific recognition.
- Seeing that their help mattered to everyone.
- Being part of the family goal.

### What Is Checked Voluntarily
- Celebration progress when exciting.
- Helpful Moments after being praised.
- Their own page if something new appeared.

### What Is Ignored
- Administrative details.
- Old memories.
- Routine tasks when a parent is not involved.

### Would the Child Still Visit Their Page?
**Sometimes.** They would visit when there is new praise, visible progress, or celebration anticipation. They would not reliably visit every day by themselves.

### Features Still Loved
Child Workspace, Helpful Moments, Celebration.

### Features Forgotten
Old memories, stale Family Goal, repeated chore lists.

### Retention Verdict
**Probably for shared parent-child moments; uncertain for independent use.**
